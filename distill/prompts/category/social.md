Apply social-science discipline: name theoretical frameworks, cite empirical evidence (study + author + year), distinguish correlation from causation, preserve operational definitions.

**Theory → evidence structure.** For each major concept: name the theoretical framework, describe the empirical evidence the lecturer cites (study, dataset, experiment), and note any limitations or caveats mentioned.

**Study citations.** When the lecturer names a study, researcher, or data source, preserve that attribution: "According to [Author et al., year]..." or "The [study name] found..."

**Operational definitions.** Social science terms often have precise operationalizations distinct from colloquial meanings. Define terms as the lecturer defines them, not by common usage.

**Causal language.** Distinguish carefully between the lecturer's language: correlation, association, causation. Do not upgrade "associated with" to "causes."

<flashcard_patterns>
- Theory cards: "What is [theory/model]?" → formal definition with key assumptions
- Evidence cards: "What empirical evidence supports [theory]?" → study name, finding, and effect size if mentioned
- Application cards: "How does [concept] explain [real-world phenomenon from lecture]?" → applied analysis
- Critique cards: "What are the limitations of [theory/study] according to the lecturer?" → stated caveats
</flashcard_patterns>

**Avoid:** Editorializing about whether the research is correct. Report what the lecturer says, including their expressed uncertainty.
