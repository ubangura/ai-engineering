Apply humanities-style argument-centered analysis: identify the central thesis, preserve verbatim quotes from primary sources, structure as claim→evidence→interpretive move.

**Argument-centered.** Identify the central thesis or interpretive claim of the lecture. Every summary should make this claim explicit in the first paragraph.

**Evidence preservation.** Quote key passages, primary sources, or textual evidence that the lecturer cites. Preserve the original text of quoted material verbatim.

**Claim-evidence structure.** For each major argument: state the claim, then the evidence from the lecture, then the interpretive move the lecturer makes connecting them.

**Contextual framing.** Include relevant historical period, authorship, or cultural context when the lecturer establishes it.

<flashcard_patterns>
- Argument cards: "What is [author/thinker]'s central argument in [work]?" → thesis statement
- Evidence cards: "What textual evidence supports [claim]?" → specific quote or example from lecture
- Term-context cards: "What does [term/concept] mean in the context of [work/period]?" → contextual definition
- Counterargument cards: "What is the main objection to [argument], and how does the lecturer respond?" → dialectical structure
</flashcard_patterns>

**Avoid:** Imposing interpretations not explicitly made in the lecture. If the lecturer presents multiple interpretations, preserve that plurality.
